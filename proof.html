How a hypothesis became a methodology — and a methodology became a movement.

The situation

In 2022, I was leading engineering and programme delivery at MassMutual Romania. The organisation was technically strong — experienced developers, capable managers, genuine ambition. But the delivery culture was fragmented. Teams worked in isolation. Agile was performed rather than practised. Change initiatives arrived, created turbulence, and dissolved without residue.

The hypothesis

I had no transformation mandate. No dedicated budget. What I had was a hypothesis: that if you identify the right people, give them a real methodology, and build a cohort around shared practice rather than shared certification, the culture shifts from within.

So I built the programme anyway.

What happened

Over six months, 98 participants across 20 engineering teams went through a structured Agile leadership curriculum designed around behaviour change, not framework literacy. The selection process mattered as much as the content. I wasn't looking for the most senior people — I was looking for the ones their peers already trusted. The ones who would carry something after the programme ended.

By the end, 23 engineers and team leads had earned Agile Champion certification — not as a credential, but as a demonstrated capability that their peers had witnessed and trusted. These weren't people who had passed a test. They were people who had changed how their teams made decisions.

What it left behind

The programme didn't end when the cohort did. The Champions became the carriers. They onboarded new team members. They facilitated retrospectives differently. They raised the floor on what good looked like — not because they were told to, but because they had the tools and the standing to do it.

That experience didn't just validate a methodology. It became the methodology.

"The question isn't whether your teams can do Agile. It's whether they can sustain it without you in the room."

DevTalks 2025

In 2025, I spoke at DevTalks — Romania's largest developer conference — about what the MassMutual programme revealed: that the missing ingredient in most Agile transformations isn't process. It's the deliberate, structured development of the people who hold the culture together when nobody's watching.

The talk drew on three years of running this kind of work — and on the honest reckoning of what failed before the methodology was right.
