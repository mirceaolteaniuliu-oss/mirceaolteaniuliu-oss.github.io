The Problem

You've done the training. You hired the consultants. Nothing changed.

You brought in a firm. They ran the workshops, certified the teams, handed over a framework, and left. Six months later, you're looking at the same delays, the same silos, the same conversations about why delivery keeps slipping. The Agile ceremonies are still on the calendar. Nobody believes in them anymore.

This isn't a skills problem. Your people are technically capable. The problem is cultural — and most transformation programmes aren't designed to fix that. They're designed to be delivered, invoiced, and renewed.

The COOs I work with have usually spent €150K to €250K before they call me. They're not sceptical about change. They're exhausted by change theatre. They want something that outlasts the engagement.

That's the only problem I solve.

The Method

The Hive Method: a six-month culture reset, built from the inside out.

External consultants bring frameworks. Frameworks don't change behaviour — people do. When the consultant leaves, the framework sits in a shared drive and the team reverts to what feels safe. The knowledge was never transferred. The ownership was never built.

I don't train your teams on Agile. I build Agile Champions inside your organisation — people who carry the methodology, model the behaviours, and hold the standard after I'm gone. The programme runs for six months, in cohort format, across your engineering and delivery teams simultaneously.

A healthy organisation, like a well-kept hive, doesn't need constant intervention. It needs the right conditions, the right structure, and people who understand their role in something larger than their own team.

FAQ

The real questions

"We've tried Agile before. Why would this be different?"

Because most Agile programmes are designed to deliver a methodology, not to change how your organisation thinks about work. The training ends, the consultants leave, and the cultural gravity pulls everyone back to what felt familiar. The Hive Method is specifically designed around that failure mode. The programme doesn't end when I leave — it ends when the internal Champions can run it without me.

"We can't justify this level of investment right now."

That's worth examining. A mid-size firm with persistent delivery problems is typically losing far more than €85K a year in delayed product, team attrition, and leadership bandwidth spent managing dysfunction rather than driving growth. The question isn't whether you can afford the programme. It's whether the status quo is actually cheaper than it appears. The Diagnostic exists precisely to make that calculus visible.

"Why you, and not a larger consultancy?"

Large consultancies optimise for repeatability. They bring the same framework to every client, delivered by whoever is available, priced to support a team of twelve. I work with a small number of organisations at any given time, which means the work is specific, not templated. You're working with the person who built the methodology, not someone trained on it. And because I don't have a renewal model to protect, my incentive is the same as yours: that you don't need me again.

The longer a culture stays stuck, the more expensive unsticking it becomes.
